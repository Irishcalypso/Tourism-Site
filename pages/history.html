Galway, the "City of the Tribes" was originally a fishing village near Galway spanish arch at the confluence of the River Corrib and the beautiful Galway Bay. The Normans came to Ireland in 1169 and began to Occupy Connacht.

It was occupied by the Anglo-Normans under Richard de Burgo until 1232. The various clans in the area built defensive walls. The work was tough, It was a tall order given the city's small population and the tools available at the time. About 1270 the wall was in place, including the Spanish Arch itself, the city began to grow and prosper. Richard II delegated the power of government to 14 merchant families, known locally as the 14 tribes from Galway. Each of the 14 tribes maintained independence while still maintaining a respectful relationship with the British crown. The city of Galway is known today as the "City of the Tribes", the names of the fourteen tribes are represented at the roundabout in Galway City.

The earliest monuments is the Turoe Stone near Loughrea. It is a piece with "Latin" Celtic art, that dates back to 200 BC. This curvilinear art is reminiscent of Ireland's Celtic past if The Latène Celts came to Ireland from Switzerland, bringing with them language, writing, folklore and music.
From the 5th century to the 10th century, Ireland was known as the land of saints and scholars, Galway No exception. Some of the Christian saints in Galway are Saint Enda of Inishmore, St Columcille, St Non-gold a Cong, St Rock at Little Killary and Saint Sourney at Inishmore.
The families had enormous influence in society in Galway and they had huge political power and wealth. Lynches castle is a prime example of their extravagance with elaborate stonework carved into the building, this was also their home.
